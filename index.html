Do you know what you're drinking?

a primer on bourbon and scotch

What is Whiskey?

Let's start with what goes into making a whisky. By definition, whiskey is a spirit distilled from fermented grain mash aged in oak barrels. Grain varieties include wheat, rye, barley, and corn. Whiskey is made all over the world, which leads to there being quite a few different varieties. The term ‘whisky’ is derived from the Gaelic ‘uisge beatha’, or ‘usquebaugh’, meaning ‘water of life’. Gaelic is that branch of Celtic spoken in the Highlands of Scotland. This brings us to the first Whiskey we will look at. You guessed it. Scotch.

Scotch

Geography is an easy way to distinguish certain types of whiskey.
Scotland, the producer of scotch, has more distilleries than any other country. You may have heard of the "single malt scotch", the most distinguished of scotches. A single malt is produced at one distillery ("single"), using only malted barley as the grain ("malt"), and is distilled in copper pot stills. The process is expensive, but the whisky produced is richly flavored and, because it's not blended with whiskies from other distilleries, very individualistic. Highland Park and The Glenlivet are popular single malt scotches.

When was Scotch Whiskey first distilled?

Whisky has been distilled in Scotland for hundreds of years. There is some evidence to show that the art of distilling could have been brought to the country by Christian missionary monks, but it has never been proved that Highland farmers did not themselves discover how to distil spirits from their surplus barley.

Blended Scotch outsells single malts by a high margin. Blending smooths out rough edges and fills in missing gaps in the whisky's flavor profile.

Side note- Scotch is historically known for a smoky flavor, known as "peaty". That flavor came from using a malted barley that was dried over a peat fire, which was once the only practical fuel source for many distilleries. These days it's used as an optional flavor enhancement, not a required method.

Bourbon

America has its own forms of whiskey, the most popular of which is Bourbon. Bourbon is America's only native spirit first created, distilled, and aged in Kentucky. The many years spent aging in new barrels is one of the reasons Kentucky Bourbon is among the best whiskies in the world today.

The production of bourbon doesn't just follow a common practice; it has to follow Bourbon law established by an act of Congress in 1964.

As of 2013, approximately 95% of all bourbon is produced in Kentucky. The state has 4.9 million barrels of bourbon that are ageing – a number that exceeds the state population.

King James IV Knows A Good Time

In 1506, King James IV of Scotland purchased large amounts of Scotch whisky from the Guild of Surgeon Barbers, a well known whisky producer at the time.

Start of something Beautiful

In 1785, Bourbon County (named after the House of Bourbon in France), was established in Kentucky. These days Bourbon County has little importance in the production of bourbon whiskey, instead most production is concentrated in areas such as Louisville, Frankfurt and Bardstown.

Johnnie Walker Shows Who's Boss

In 1820, John Walker started production of his famous blended Scotch whisky. Today, this brand of whisky holds the record as the most popular and largest selling Scotch whisky in the world!

Mr. Beam

The Beam family (originally spelled Böhm) moved from Germany to Kentucky in the late 18th century. In 1795, Jacob Beam retailed his first barrel of Bourbon whiskey. The family legacy continues today and is now on its 7th generation.
