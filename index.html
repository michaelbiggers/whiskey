Do you know what you're drinking?

a primer on bourbon and scotch

What is Whiskey?

Let's start with what goes into making a whisky. By definition, whiskey is a spirit distilled from fermented grain mash aged in oak barrels. Grain varieties include wheat, rye, barley, and corn. Whiskey is made all over the world, which leads to there being quite a few different varieties. Bourbon and Scotch are two whiskies with a lot of similarities, but they each have a story of their own. Let's learn the differences between them and become closer with the spirits.

Geography is an easy way to distinguish certain types of whiskey.

Scotland, the producer of scotch, has more distilleries than any other country. You may have heard of the "single malt scotch", the most distinguished of scotches. A single malt is produced at one distillery ("single"), using only malted barley as the grain ("malt"), and is distilled in copper pot stills. The process is expensive, but the whisky produced is richly flavored and, because it's not blended with whiskies from other distilleries, very individualistic. Highland Park and The Glenlivet are popular single malt scotches.

Blended scotch outsells single malts by a high margin. Blending smooths out rough edges and fills in missing gaps in the whisky's flavor profile.

Scotch is historically known for a smoky flavor, known as "peaty". That flavor came from using a malted barley that was dried over a peat fire, which was once the only practical fuel source for many distilleries. These days it's used as an optional flavor enhancement, not a required method.

Bourbon

America has its own forms of whiskey, the most popular of which is Bourbon. Bourbon is America's only native spirit first created, distilled, and aged in Kentucky. The many years spent aging in new barrels is one of the reasons Kentucky Bourbon is among the best whiskies in the world today.

The production of bourbon doesn't just follow a common practice; it has to follow Bourbon law.

As of 2013, approximately 95% of all bourbon is produced in Kentucky. The state has 4.9 million barrels of bourbon that are ageing – a number that exceeds the state population.

There are plenty of other examples - Canadian whiskey, Irish scotch, Tenessee whiskey. But with the information you've learned here, you're well-equipped to walk into a bar and order a bourbon, neat, and have an idea of what you're getting. Bon voyage, whiskey friend.
