What is WHiskey?

Let's start with what goes into making a whisky. By definition, whiskey (or whisky, in Scotland) is a spirit distilled from fermented grain mash — grain varieties include wheat, rye, barley, and corn — and then aged in oak barrels. Whiskey is made all over the world, which leads to there being quite a few different varieties. Let's take a look at some of the differences.

History

Bourbon

Kentucky Bourbon is America's only native spirit. First created, distilled, and aged in Kentucky. The invention of bourbon is often attributed to a pioneering minister and distiller named Elijah Craig. He is said to also be the first to age the distillation in charred oak casks, "a process that gives the bourbon its reddish color and unique taste". The many years spent aging in new barrels is one of the reasons Kentucky Bourbon is among the best whiskies in the world today.

As of 2013, approximately 95% of all bourbon is produced in Kentucky. The state has 4.9 million barrels of bourbon that are aging – a number that exceeds the state population.
